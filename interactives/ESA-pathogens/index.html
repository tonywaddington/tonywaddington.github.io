Tears and some other body fluids such as saliva contain an enzyme called lysozyme which digests the cell walls of many bacteria.

The lining of the nose, trachea and bronchi secretes a layer of mucus that traps bacteria and dust. Tiny cilia beat to push the trapped pathogens up to the nose or throat, from where they are blown out of the nose or swallowed.

Sweat that is acidic and contains lysozyme.

The stomach secretes an extremely acidic gastric juice (pH 1-2) which kills most bacteria (though not agents of food-borne diseases).

Commensals in colon.

Blood clots that form when the skin is broken.

Interferon is a protein produced by cells infected by viruses - it acts to prevent reproduction of viruses after entry to a host cell.

Acidic conditions in the vagina inhibit the growth of many micro-organisms.

Flushing of urinary tract.

The outer layer of skin (epidermis) contains a tough, water resistant protein called keratin. The greasy sebum, secreted by the sebaceous glands, has an anti-microbial effect, as does the wax secreted by the skin lining the outer ear cavity.
