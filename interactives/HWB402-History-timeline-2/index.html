Views changed from:

Thinking that tāngata whairoa with mental illness were ‘lunatics’ and should be confined in gaols and, later, purpose-built asylums.

Supporting them to live well in their community with natural support, acknowledging people’s’ rights to have hope and belong to the culture and lifestyle they identify with.

1840–
1900

By the late nineteenth century, people with mental illness were institutionalised in public asylums in New Zealand. Asylums were also used to confine categories of people outcast by society – for example, elderly people who were suffering from mild confusion.

In the early days of institutionalisation, no distinction was made between people with severe mental illness and those who were temporarily unwell.

People of all ages, including children, were held together in the same areas. The focus was on their long-term care and custody rather than attempts to treat them and help their recovery.

Patients in asylums had no rights.

1900–
1970

The asylum-based model of containment for people who were mentally ill was replaced between 1910 and 1930 by a different model – that of mental hospitals. ‘Lunatics’ became inmates and female attendants became nurses in an attempt to professionalise the hospitals. Attempts were made to build some new, more pleasant mental hospitals, but most people remained in the nineteenth century asylum buildings.

New experimental treatments were forced on people with severe mental illness, producing terrible side effects, even death, and these were discontinued after a few years. Early forms of psychotherapy were used successfully with soldiers returning from WWI suffering from shell shock.

Mental hospitals were replaced between the 1940s and the 1960s by a different view of treating mental illness – psychiatric hospitals. These began to use new and powerful drugs to treat people with severe mental illness, together with improved psychotherapy approaches. The new treatments encouraged voluntary admissions and allowed for shorter hospital stays.

Long-term stay patients had no rights and no improvement in conditions.

1970–
2000

As part of the movement of community-based services that grew in the 1970s, most tāngata whaiora with mental illness were now receiving support from a range of non-governmental organisations (NGOs). These NGOs increasingly offered alternatives to institutional care. Nearly all psychiatric hospitals had closed by the 1990s.

The majority of funding for mental health services was gradually transferred to NGOs.

However, not all tāngata whaiora who had been institutionalised for long periods of time could cope with community care, and so boarding house accommodation and sheltered housing had to be found for them.

With the deinstitutionalisation of the psychiatric hospitals came stories of abuse suffered by tāngata whaiora. These were presented to a confidential forum, but had little response from government.

The 1996 Mason Report was a review of mental health services in New Zealand. The report was instrumental in the Mental Health Commission being established in 1996 to advise government on the needs of tāngata whaiora and to advocate for better promotion of mental health and services.

The Mason Report led to the integration of drug and alcohol policy and services with those for mental health issues.

2000
onwards

From 2000 onwards the recovery movement has given support to tāngata whaiora on their journey to wellness.

Concerns about underfunding of NGO services for mental health and addiction services has been a concern since 2000.
